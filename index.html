Route 66 — The Mother Road

🛣️ Chicago to Santa Monica — 2,448 Miles of American History

Route 66, known as the "Mother Road" and the "Main Street of America," is the most iconic highway in the world. Established in 1926, it stretches 2,448 miles through 8 states — Illinois, Missouri, Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and California — connecting the heartland to the Pacific coast. Decommissioned in 1985, it lives on as a symbol of freedom, adventure, and the open road.

📍 The Route

From Chicago, Illinois to Santa Monica, California — passing through the Great Plains, the Ozarks, the Texas Panhandle, the New Mexico desert, the Arizona highlands, and the Mojave Desert before reaching the Pacific Ocean.

📅 1926 — Birth of the Mother Road

Route 66 was officially established on November 11, 1926. It was one of the first numbered highways in the US Highway System, designed to connect rural communities to urban centers and provide a reliable cross-country route.

🌾 1930s — The Dust Bowl Migration

During the Great Depression and the Dust Bowl of the 1930s, Route 66 became the primary escape route for hundreds of thousands of displaced families fleeing Oklahoma, Texas, and Kansas for California. John Steinbeck immortalized this exodus in The Grapes of Wrath (1939), calling Route 66 "the Mother Road."

🚗 1950s — Golden Age of Road Travel

Post-WWII prosperity and the rise of the automobile made Route 66 the ultimate road trip destination. Iconic diners, neon-lit motels, drive-in theaters, and roadside attractions flourished. Bobby Troup's 1946 song "(Get Your Kicks on) Route 66" — covered by Nat King Cole, the Rolling Stones, and Depeche Mode — made it a global symbol of freedom.

🛣️ 1985 — Decommissioned

The Interstate Highway System gradually replaced Route 66, and on June 27, 1985, it was officially removed from the US Highway System. But the road refused to die. Preservation groups, nostalgic travelers, and international tourists kept the spirit alive — and today Historic Route 66 is one of the most visited tourist routes in the world.

🍔 Vintage Americana

The soul of Route 66 lives in its diners, motels, and roadside art. Delgadillo's Snow Cap Drive-In in Seligman, the Blue Swallow Motel in Tucumcari, the Wigwam Motel in Holbrook, and the Cadillac Ranch in Amarillo are just a few of the legendary stops that define the authentic Route 66 experience.

🎬 Route 66 in Pop Culture

Route 66 has inspired countless films, TV shows, songs, and books. Pixar's Cars (2006) was directly modeled after Seligman, AZ. The TV series Route 66 (1960–1964) filmed on location along the highway. Easy Rider (1969), National Lampoon's Vacation (1983), and Rain Man (1988) all featured iconic Route 66 scenes.

🛍️ Shopping & Souvenirs

Route 66 is a treasure trove for collectors and souvenir hunters. Antique shops, trading posts, and roadside stands sell everything from vintage Route 66 signs and license plates to Native American jewelry and hand-painted pottery. Every stop has something unique that you won't find anywhere else.

Seligman, Arizona

🛣️ The Birthplace of Historic Route 66

Seligman, Arizona is the town that saved Route 66. When Interstate 40 bypassed the town in 1978, local barber Angel Delgadillo rallied residents and founded the Historic Route 66 Association of Arizona. By 1987, Seligman was officially designated the "Birthplace of Historic Route 66" — the spark that ignited the global Route 66 revival. The town also inspired Pixar's beloved film Cars (2006), with Seligman serving as the real-world model for Radiator Springs.

🍦 Delgadillo's Snow Cap

Built in 1953 by Juan Delgadillo from scrap railroad lumber, the Snow Cap Drive-In is the most iconic stop on all of Route 66. Famous for its irreverent humor, fake mustard squirt guns, and the best soft-serve in Arizona. Juan passed away in 2004 but his family keeps the spirit alive.

✂️ Angel Delgadillo's Barbershop

Angel Delgadillo — the "Guardian Angel of Route 66" — still greets visitors at his historic barbershop on Route 66. Now a gift shop and museum, it's the spiritual heart of the Route 66 preservation movement. Angel was inducted into the Route 66 Hall of Fame in 2001.

🚗 Cars Movie Connection

Pixar's John Lasseter drove Route 66 in 2000 and was so moved by Seligman that he modeled Radiator Springs directly after it. The Lightning McQueen #95 art car and other Cars-themed vehicles are displayed along the main street as a tribute to this cinematic legacy.

🏛️ Historic Main Street

Seligman's main street (Historic Route 66 / Chino Street) is lined with vintage motels, neon signs, antique shops, and art installations. The Rusty Bolt, Roadkill Café, and Juan's Garden are among the colorful stops that make every visit feel like stepping back into 1955.

📍 Getting There

Seligman sits at Mile Marker 121 on Historic Route 66 in Yavapai County, Arizona. From Flagstaff, head west on I-40 and take Exit 123 — then follow the brown Historic Route 66 signs. The town is 90 miles east of Kingman and 75 miles west of Williams.

🌵 Best Time to Visit

Spring (March–May) and Fall (September–November) offer the best weather. Summer temperatures can reach 100°F. The annual Route 66 Fun Run in late April draws thousands of classic car enthusiasts through Seligman every year.

Nelson Ghost Town, Nevada

⛏️ Eldorado Canyon — Nevada's Most Lawless Mining Town

Nelson, Nevada — also known as El Dorado — sits deep in Eldorado Canyon, about 45 minutes south of Las Vegas along the Colorado River. The canyon was named in 1857 by steamboat captain George Johnson when gold and silver were discovered here. By 1861, the Techatticup Mine was established, sparking one of the most violent and lawless gold rushes in Nevada history. Murders, claim-jumping, and deserting Civil War soldiers made this one of the most dangerous places in the American West. Today, Nelson is a beloved ghost town and filming location — featured in music videos, films, and video games — and is open to visitors year-round.

💰 Techatticup Mine

Established in 1861, the Techatticup Mine was the most productive gold and silver mine in southern Nevada. It operated through the 1940s and produced millions in ore. Today, guided underground tours take visitors through the original mine shafts, revealing the brutal working conditions and violent history of the canyon.

💀 Murder, Mayhem & Deserters

During the Civil War, deserters from both Union and Confederate armies flooded Eldorado Canyon. The remote location made law enforcement nearly impossible. Claim-jumping and murder were common. The canyon earned a reputation as one of the most dangerous places in the West — even the U.S. Army was afraid to enter.

🎥 Hollywood Ghost Town

Nelson has been featured in dozens of music videos (including Beyoncé's "Hold Up"), films, and video games. The rusted vintage cars, crumbling adobe buildings, and crashed 1940s airplane wreckage make it one of the most photogenic locations in the American Southwest.

🛸 The Crashed Airplane

One of Nelson's most iconic features is a vintage WWII-era airplane wreck that sits in the canyon. The Stearman biplane crashed in the 1940s and has remained there ever since, slowly being reclaimed by the desert. It is one of the most photographed objects in all of Nevada.

📍 Getting There

Nelson Ghost Town is located at 16880 State Hwy 165, Nelson, NV 89046, approximately 45 minutes south of Las Vegas. Take US-95 South to NV-165 East (Nelson Cutoff Road) and follow it into Eldorado Canyon. Open 24 hours. Admission is free; guided mine tours are available for a fee.

📸 What to See

Beyond the mine, visitors can explore rusted vintage cars from the 1940s–60s, original mining equipment, adobe ruins, the crashed airplane, and stunning views of Eldorado Canyon and the Colorado River. Sunrise and golden hour photography here is extraordinary.

Kingman, Arizona

🛣️ Heart of Historic Route 66 — Arizona's Longest Preserved Stretch

Kingman, Arizona proudly calls itself the "Heart of Historic Route 66." The city boasts the longest remaining preserved stretch of Route 66 in the entire country — a section that runs right through its historic downtown along Andy Devine Avenue. Named after beloved Hollywood actor and Kingman native Andy Devine, this stretch of road is lined with vintage motels, neon signs, murals, and classic diners. Kingman is also the gateway to Oatman (the wild burro town) and sits at the crossroads of the Mojave Desert and the Black Mountains.

🏛️ Historic Powerhouse Visitor Center

The crown jewel of Kingman's Route 66 heritage. The 1907 Powerhouse building — built for nearly $300,000 — now houses the Arizona Route 66 Museum, the Route 66 Electric Vehicle Museum, and the Guardian Angel of Route 66 StoryFile Experience. It's the best Route 66 museum in Arizona.

🎬 Andy Devine — Hollywood's Kingman Son

Andy Devine (1905–1977) was born in Flagstaff but raised in Kingman. He became one of Hollywood's most beloved character actors, known for his distinctive raspy voice and roles in westerns alongside John Wayne. Andy Devine Avenue (Route 66 through downtown) is named in his honor.

🦎 Oatman — The Wild Burro Town

Just 28 miles west of Kingman on the original Route 66 mountain road lies Oatman, a former gold mining town now famous for its wild burros that roam the streets freely. The drive through the Black Mountains on old Route 66 is one of the most spectacular and challenging stretches of the entire highway.

🍽️ Dining & Neon Signs

Kingman's downtown is a feast for Route 66 lovers. Mr. D'z Route 66 Diner serves classic American food under iconic pink neon. The Dambar Steakhouse has been feeding travelers since 1937. Vintage neon signs from the 1950s and 60s still glow along Andy Devine Avenue at night.

📍 Getting There

Kingman is located at the junction of I-40 and US-93 in Mohave County, Arizona. It's 30 miles east of the Nevada border, 100 miles south of Las Vegas, and 150 miles northwest of Phoenix. The historic downtown is centered on Andy Devine Avenue (Route 66) and Beale Street.

🌅 Sunsets & Stargazing

Kingman sits at 3,336 feet elevation in the high Mojave Desert. The sunsets over the Hualapai Mountains are legendary among Route 66 travelers. The low light pollution makes Kingman one of the best places in Arizona for stargazing — the Milky Way is clearly visible on clear nights.

Cathedral Gorge State Park, Nevada

🏜️ Maze-Like Spires · Ancient Clay · Eerie Silence

Cathedral Gorge State Park in Lincoln County, Nevada, is a geological wonder sculpted over five million years from soft bentonite clay. Ancient volcanic ash settled in a prehistoric lake, compacted into the Panaca Formation, and was then carved by wind and rain into a haunting labyrinth of slot canyons, towering spires, and hoodoos. The Southern Paiute people knew this land long before European settlers arrived in the 1880s. Nevada Governor James Scrugham acquired the land in 1924, and it became one of Nevada's first four state parks in 1935, developed by the Civilian Conservation Corps.

🏛️ The Geology

Five million years ago, this valley was a freshwater lake. Volcanic ash and debris settled and compacted into soft bentonite clay — the Panaca Formation. As the lake drained, relentless erosion by wind and rain sculpted the maze of slot canyons, cathedral-like spires, and hoodoos you see today. The formations are constantly, slowly changing.

🕳️ The Slot Canyons

The park's most striking feature is its system of narrow, winding slot canyons. As you descend into these passages, the towering walls close in and the light dims to an eerie glow. The famous "Rabbit Hole" tunnel leads to a hidden chamber. Sound behaves strangely here — sometimes amplified, sometimes eerily absorbed. After dark, the canyons feel genuinely otherworldly.

🌌 Dark Sky Stargazing

Cathedral Gorge is a designated International Dark Sky Park. Far from city lights in the remote Nevada desert, the Milky Way blazes overhead on clear nights. The combination of the alien landscape below and the star-filled sky above creates an experience unlike anywhere else. Bring a blanket, lie on the desert floor, and let the universe swallow you whole.

👻 Eerie Atmosphere

No documented ghost stories — but Cathedral Gorge doesn't need them. The maze-like canyons disorient visitors. The silence is absolute. The clay walls seem to absorb sound. At dusk, the spires cast long, shifting shadows that resemble figures. The Piano Guys filmed their "Sweet Child O' Mine" video here, and the 2000 film "The Last Real Cowboys" used it as a backdrop.

📍 How to Get There

Located along U.S. Route 93, approximately 2 miles northwest of Panaca, Nevada. From Las Vegas: take I-15 North to US-93 North (about 2.5 hours). From Salt Lake City: take I-15 South to US-93 South. The park entrance is well-signed. Camping available on-site.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

Spring and Fall for the most comfortable temperatures. Summer highs reach 95°F (35°C) but the canyon walls provide shade. Winter nights drop to 55°F (13°C). Dawn and dusk offer the most dramatic light on the formations. The park is open year-round, 24 hours a day.

Goblin Valley State Park, Utah

🛸 Alien Landscape · Mushroom Rocks · Dark Sky Park

Goblin Valley State Park, near Hanksville, Utah, is one of the most bizarre landscapes on Earth. Thousands of mushroom-shaped sandstone formations — called "goblins" — fill a hidden valley in the San Rafael Swell. Cowboys discovered it in the late 1920s; Arthur Chaffin photographed it in 1949 and called it the "Valley of the Mushrooms." It became a state park in 1964. In 2013, Boy Scout leaders deliberately toppled a 200-million-year-old hoodoo, sparking national outrage and criminal charges. The park is also an International Dark Sky Park.

🍄 The Goblins

Unlike most parks where you stay on trails, Goblin Valley invites you to wander freely among the formations. The "goblins" — thousands of them — range from knee-height to towering 10-foot figures. Each one is unique, shaped by 200 million years of erosion. At sunset, they cast long shadows that make them look alive. Visitors consistently describe the valley as feeling like another planet.

🌌 Dark Sky Stargazing

Goblin Valley is one of Utah's most remote locations, with virtually zero light pollution. The park is a certified International Dark Sky Park. On clear nights, the Milky Way is so bright it casts shadows among the goblins. The combination of the alien landscape and the star-filled sky is genuinely surreal — one of the most otherworldly experiences in the American West.

🎬 Galaxy Quest & Hollywood

Goblin Valley was used as the alien planet in the 1999 film Galaxy Quest. Portions of John Carter and City Slickers II were also filmed in the area. The landscape is so otherworldly that filmmakers consistently use it to represent alien worlds. In 2013, a Scout leader's vandalism of a 200-million-year-old hoodoo made national headlines and resulted in criminal charges.

🧗 Goblin's Lair

Beyond the main valley, adventurous visitors can rappel into "Goblin's Lair" — a hidden slot canyon accessible only by rope. The cave system beneath the valley is dark, narrow, and disorienting. Guided rappelling tours are available. The Three Valleys area offers miles of exploration among increasingly bizarre formations. Mountain biking and disc golf are also available in the park.

📍 How to Get There

Located 12 miles south of Hanksville, Utah. From I-70, take Highway 24 south for 24 miles, then turn at the Temple Mountain junction and follow signs for 12 miles to the park. Approximately 216 miles southeast of Salt Lake City. Day use fee applies; camping available.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

Spring (March–May) and Fall (September–November) offer the best temperatures for exploring. Summer daytime highs reach 90–105°F (32–41°C) but evenings cool rapidly to 50°F (10°C). Winter offers solitude and occasional snow that makes the goblins look even more alien. The park is open year-round.

Lake Dolores / Rock-A-Hoola Waterpark, California

💀 Abandoned · Cursed · Mojave Desert Ghost

In the heart of the Mojave Desert near Newberry Springs, California, the skeletal ruins of Lake Dolores Waterpark rot under the desert sun. Built by Bob Byers for his wife Dolores in the early 1960s, the park opened in 1962 using underground springs from the Mojave Aquifer. It thrived through the 1970s and early 1980s before closing. Reopened as Rock-A-Hoola in 1998, it went bankrupt in 2000 after a paralysis lawsuit. Briefly reopened as Discovery Waterpark in 2002–2004, it has been abandoned ever since — vandalized, burned, and slowly consumed by the desert.

💀 The Cursed History

The park has cycled through three names and three bankruptcies. In 1999, an employee was paralyzed in an accident, triggering a $4.4 million lawsuit that broke the park financially. Dolores Byers, the woman the park was named after, died in 2001 — one month after selling the property. In 2018, an arson fire destroyed the Lazy River Cafe and Arcade. Every attempt to revive it has ended in failure.

👻 Haunted Ruins

The abandoned gift shop is rumored to be haunted by a painting of a woman that taggers cannot paint over — the paint never stays. Visitors have conducted séances between the former arcade and gift shop. An eerie, sudden cold wind has been reported even when the air was previously still and hot. The skeletal waterslides loom over the desert like the bones of a dead giant.

🎬 Pop Culture

Boards of Canada debuted their album Tomorrow\'s Harvest here in 2013. Kesha filmed her "High Road" music video here in 2020. BTS filmed a mini concert here in 2022. Tony Hawk appeared in a Mini Cooper commercial filmed at the ruins. The site has been featured in Abandoned (Viceland), Rob & Big (MTV), and BuzzFeed Unsolved. The 1998 film Desert Blue was filmed here.

📸 Urban Exploration

The ruins are a magnet for urban explorers and photographers. The graffiti-covered waterslides, the desolate lake bed, the burned-out arcade, and the post-apocalyptic aesthetic make it one of the most photogenic abandoned sites in California. The contrast of the colorful graffiti against the stark Mojave Desert is visually stunning. Note: the site is private property with "No Trespassing" signs.

📍 How to Get There

Located off Interstate 15 near Newberry Springs, California. Take the Hacienda Road exit from I-15. Approximately 27 miles northeast of Barstow and 100 miles southwest of Las Vegas. The ruins are visible from the highway. The property is privately owned — visit at your own risk.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

Late fall, winter, and early spring (November to April) to avoid the extreme Mojave summer heat which can exceed 115°F (46°C). Dawn and dusk offer the most dramatic light on the ruins. Night photography is particularly striking but the hazardous conditions of an abandoned site make daytime visits safer.

Lafayette, Colorado — The Vampire's Town

🧛 Coal Town · Transylvania · The Lafayette Vampire

Lafayette, Colorado, 10 miles east of Boulder, was founded as a coal town in 1888 by Mary E. Miller, named after her late husband. Romanian and Eastern European immigrants flooded in to work the mines, bringing their Old World superstitions with them. When Theodore "Fodor" Glava — a gaunt Transylvanian coal miner — died in 1918 during the Spanish Flu epidemic, terrified locals convinced themselves he was a vampire. What followed was one of the strangest events in Colorado history.

🧛 The Lafayette Vampire

Theodore "Fodor" Glava arrived from Transylvania, Romania, and worked the coal mines. When he died in 1918 — gaunt, pale, and foreign — superstitious locals decided he was a vampire. After his burial in Lafayette Cemetery, rumors spread of livestock dying and a shadowy figure wandering the town. Terrified townsfolk dug up his grave, misread natural decomposition as vampirism, and drove a wooden stake through his heart. A tree now grows from the center of his grave — said to have sprouted from that very stake.

⛏️ Coal Mining History

Lafayette was built on coal. Miners from Romania, Italy, and Eastern Europe worked brutal underground conditions. The Coal Creek area is said to be haunted by the spirits of miners who perished underground. Ghost hunters report apparitions, sudden cold rushes of wind, and the feeling of being followed near the cemetery and old mining sites. The mine tunnels were also used for illicit Prohibition-era bootlegging.

🌳 The Vampire's Grave

Theodore Glava's grave in Lafayette Cemetery is marked by a tree growing directly from its center — the legendary stake that killed the vampire, now grown into a full tree. The grave is a pilgrimage site for paranormal enthusiasts. The cemetery itself is one of the most atmospheric in Colorado, with Victorian-era headstones, old mining family plots, and the lingering weight of a community that lived and died hard.

👻 The Shadow That Never Died

Our YouTube video "The Lafayette Shadow That Never Died" explores the full legend — from the Transylvanian connection to the coal mines to the grave with the tree. The shadow of Fodor Glava has never fully left Lafayette. Locals still tell the story. The cemetery still draws visitors who come to see the tree growing from the vampire's heart. The Coal Creek trail at night is not for the faint of heart.

📍 How to Get There

Lafayette is located approximately 22 miles northwest of Denver. From Denver: take I-25 North to US-36 West (Denver-Boulder Turnpike), then exit for CO-121/Wadsworth Parkway north, which becomes US-287 North into Lafayette. The Lafayette Cemetery is on Baseline Road, just east of US-287. Free and open to the public.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

Late October evenings or foggy autumn mornings for the most atmospheric experience. The cemetery is most haunting at dawn or dusk when mist rises from Coal Creek. The Lafayette History Museum and historic downtown are worth visiting year-round. Ghost tours are available in October.

Bunkerville, Nevada

🌵 Mormon Settlement · Nuclear Fallout · Virgin River Gorge

Bunkerville, Nevada, was settled in 1877 by Mormon pioneer Edward Bunker, who moved his polygamous family here after a communal settlement in Utah failed. The town sits along the Virgin River near the Nevada-Arizona border. In the 1950s, Bunkerville was directly downwind of nuclear test sites — residents recall playing in what they thought was snow, but was actually nuclear fallout. The legacy of childhood leukemia and government distrust runs deep. In 2014, the town made national news during the Bundy standoff.

🏠 Mormon Pioneer History

Edward Bunker led his family here in 1877 with permission from Brigham Young to establish a new communal experiment — the United Order. Residents shared work, land, and the fruits of their labor. The experiment ended in 1880, but the Bunker family stayed. The Edward Bunker Home still stands as a historic landmark. The town retains its deep Mormon heritage and frontier character.

☢️ Nuclear Downwinders

In the 1950s, the U.S. government conducted nuclear tests at the Nevada Test Site, 100 miles northwest. Bunkerville was directly in the fallout path. Residents — including children — played in the white powder that drifted down, not knowing it was radioactive. Childhood leukemia rates spiked. The community became known as "Downwinders" — citizens poisoned by their own government's secret tests. The distrust of federal authority that began then never fully healed.

🔥 Little Finland & Devil's Throat

Near Bunkerville lies "Little Finland" (also called Hobgoblin's Playground and Devil's Fire) — a surreal landscape of red and orange sandstone formations that look like something from another world. Nearby, Devil's Throat is a massive sinkhole that drops 130 feet straight down into darkness. No one knows exactly how deep it goes. Both sites are accessible via dirt roads and are among the most otherworldly landscapes in Nevada.

🤠 The Bundy Standoff (2014)

In April 2014, Bunkerville became the center of a national standoff when rancher Cliven Bundy refused to pay 20 years of federal grazing fees. Armed militia members from across the country converged on the town. Federal agents backed down. The standoff became a flashpoint in the ongoing conflict between Western ranchers and the federal government — a conflict with roots going back to the nuclear downwinder era and beyond.

📍 How to Get There

Bunkerville is located in Clark County, Nevada, along the Virgin River near Mesquite. From Las Vegas: take I-15 North approximately 80 miles to the Mesquite/Bunkerville exit. Little Finland is accessible via dirt roads northeast of Bunkerville — a high-clearance vehicle is recommended. The Virgin River Gorge scenic drive on I-15 passes directly through the area.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

October through April to avoid the extreme desert heat. Summer temperatures regularly exceed 110°F (43°C). Little Finland and Devil's Throat are best visited in the morning before temperatures peak. The Virgin River Gorge is spectacular year-round, especially during spring wildflower season.

Mount Rushmore, South Dakota

🗿 Sacred Lakota Land · Stolen Hills · Controversial Monument

Mount Rushmore — originally Tunkasila Sakpe Paha (Six Grandfathers Mountain) to the Lakota people — stands in the sacred Black Hills of South Dakota. The 1868 Treaty of Fort Laramie guaranteed these hills to the Lakota forever. In 1877, after gold was discovered, the U.S. government seized the land. In 1927, sculptor Gutzon Borglum — who had documented ties to the Ku Klux Klan — began carving four presidents into the Lakota's sacred mountain. The Lakota sued, won $17.1 million in a 1980 Supreme Court ruling, and refused the money — they want their land back.

🏔️ Six Grandfathers Mountain

The Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho peoples considered this mountain sacred — a spiritual center of the Black Hills. The 1868 Treaty of Fort Laramie guaranteed the Black Hills to the Lakota "for as long as the grass shall grow." Gold was discovered in 1874. By 1877, the U.S. government had seized the land. The carving of four American presidents into this sacred mountain remains one of the most controversial acts of cultural desecration in American history.

🎨 The Sculptor's Dark Past

Gutzon Borglum, who designed and led the carving from 1927 until his death in 1941, had documented ties to the Ku Klux Klan. He previously worked on Stone Mountain in Georgia — a Confederate monument — before being fired. His son Lincoln Borglum completed Mount Rushmore. The Hall of Records tunnel, intended to house American historical artifacts, was never completed. Borglum's unfinished vision adds an element of hidden secrets to the monument.

🎬 North by Northwest & Pop Culture

Alfred Hitchcock's 1959 thriller North by Northwest features the famous chase scene at Mount Rushmore — though much of it was filmed on a scaled replica after the National Park Service refused access. The monument also appears in National Treasure: Book of Secrets. The Presidents of the United States of America performed live here for MTV in 1996. It remains one of the most photographed monuments in America.

👻 Black Hills Hauntings

The Black Hills region surrounding Mount Rushmore is rich with paranormal activity. Keystone Cemetery, just miles away, is noted for ghost sightings. The Alex Johnson Hotel in Rapid City is famously haunted by a "Lady in White" and the ghost of Alex Johnson himself. Deadwood — 25 miles north — is legendary for hauntings: Wild Bill Hickok and Calamity Jane are said to haunt Mount Moriah Cemetery. The entire Black Hills region carries the weight of a violent, unresolved past.

📍 How to Get There

Located in the Black Hills, 25 miles southwest of Rapid City, South Dakota. From I-90, exit at Rapid City and follow U.S. Highway 16 to Keystone, then SD Highway 244 to the memorial. From the south, follow U.S. Highway 385 north to SD Highway 244. Parking fee applies; the monument itself is free to visit.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

Spring through fall, especially September, for mild weather and fewer crowds. Visit before 9 AM or after 3:30 PM during peak summer months. The Evening Lighting Ceremony (Memorial Day through Labor Day) is spectacular — the faces are illuminated against the night sky. Winter visits offer solitude and dramatic snow-covered scenery.

Sturgis, South Dakota

🏍️ Motorcycle Rally · Black Hills · Wild West · Bear Butte

Sturgis, South Dakota, was founded in 1876 and named after Major Samuel D. Sturgis of Fort Meade Cavalry Post. The city is home to the world's largest motorcycle rally — the Sturgis Motorcycle Rally — which began in 1938 as a 9-person dirt track race and now draws hundreds of thousands annually. The Black Hills surrounding Sturgis are steeped in Wild West history, Native American legend, and the ghosts of gold rush towns. Nearby Deadwood is one of the most haunted cities in America.

🏍️ The Motorcycle Rally

The Sturgis Motorcycle Rally began on August 14, 1938, when Jackpine Gypsies Motorcycle Club member "Pappy" Hoel organized a race with just 9 participants. Today it draws 500,000+ riders annually, transforming the quiet Black Hills town into a roaring spectacle of chrome and leather. The rally runs for 10 days every August. Main Street becomes one of the most electric stretches of road in America — and one of the loudest.

🐻 Bear Butte — Sacred Mountain

Northeast of Sturgis rises Bear Butte — "Mato Paha" (Sleeping Bear) to the Lakota Sioux. This ancient volcanic formation is one of the most sacred sites in Native American culture. The Lakota believe it is the sleeping bear that made the scratches on Devil's Tower. Vision quests and ceremonies have been held here for thousands of years. Colorful prayer ties flutter from the trees on the summit trail. The spiritual weight of the place is palpable.

💀 Deadwood — The Haunted City

Just 14 miles from Sturgis, Deadwood is legendary for its lawless past and its ghosts. Wild Bill Hickok was shot in the back here in 1876 while holding "Dead Man's Hand" (aces and eights). He and Calamity Jane are buried at Mount Moriah Cemetery, where visitors report whispers and cold spots near their graves. Seth Bullock, Deadwood's first sheriff, is said to haunt the historic Bullock Hotel. The entire town is a living ghost story.

🪖 Fort Meade & The Star-Spangled Banner

Fort Meade, established in 1878 to protect Black Hills settlers, holds a remarkable distinction: the "Star-Spangled Banner" was first used for military ceremonies here in 1892, long before it became the national anthem. The fort also housed Comanche — the sole cavalry survivor of the Battle of Little Big Horn — for nine years. During WWII, some of its stone buildings were constructed by prisoners of war. The fort is now a Veterans Administration complex.

📍 How to Get There

Sturgis is located in Meade County, South Dakota, in the Black Hills region. From Rapid City: take I-90 West approximately 30 miles to Exit 32 or 30 for Sturgis. From Denver: take I-25 North to I-90 West (approximately 6 hours). Deadwood is 14 miles west on US-14A. Mount Rushmore is 25 miles south via US-385.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

August for the Motorcycle Rally (second week of August). Late spring or early fall for milder weather, fewer crowds, and the full Black Hills experience. The Black Hills are spectacular in fall foliage (late September–October). Winter is cold but the snow-covered hills and empty roads have their own haunting beauty.

Mormon Rocks, California

🪨 Cajon Pass · Chanting Winds · San Andreas Fault

Mormon Rocks are massive sandstone formations rising dramatically from the Cajon Pass in San Bernardino County, California. Named after the approximately 500 Mormon pioneers who passed through in 1851 en route to establish San Bernardino, these ancient rocks were uplifted by a sub-fault of the San Andreas Fault. Older maps called them the "Rock Candy Mountains." A Mormon Trail Monument (California Historic Landmark No. 577) stands nearby. Local legend calls them the "Chanting Rocks" — the wind passing through natural portholes creates sounds like low, eerie chanting.

🪨 The Geology

Mormon Rocks are part of the Transverse Ranges, a region dramatically shaped by the San Andreas Fault system. The massive sandstone formations were uplifted by a sub-fault of the San Andreas — the same fault that will one day trigger the "Big One." The rocks show deep erosion grooves, natural portholes, and caves formed over millions of years. They sit directly on one of the most seismically active zones in North America.

🎵 The Chanting Rocks

Local legend calls Mormon Rocks the "Chanting Rocks." Wind passing through the natural portholes and caves in the sandstone creates sounds akin to low chanting or singing — an eerie, rhythmic sound that visitors describe as both beautiful and unsettling. UFO sightings have been reported in the area. Whispers of ancient spirits persist among local hikers. The rocks seem to have a voice of their own.

⛪ Mormon Pioneer History

In June 1851, approximately 500 Mormon pioneers passed through Cajon Pass, guided by Amasa Lyman and Charles Rich, to establish a new settlement in San Bernardino. The Mormon Trail Monument (California Historic Landmark No. 577) stands on CA-138, 3.6 miles west of I-15. In 1857, during the Utah War, Brigham Young ordered roughly half of the San Bernardino settlers to return to Utah — a mass abandonment that left the community fractured.

🦅 Wildlife & Nature

Mormon Rocks are a birdwatcher's paradise. White-throated Swifts nest in the rock faces. Hawks and ravens circle overhead. California Thrashers, Western Kingbirds, and Coast Horned Lizards inhabit the chaparral below. The Mormon Rocks Interpretive Trail winds through the formations, offering close-up views of the sandstone faces, natural caves, and the dramatic Cajon Pass landscape.

📍 How to Get There

Located in Cajon Pass, approximately 1 mile west of Interstate 15, north of Highway 138. The Mormon Rocks Interpretive Trailhead is at the Mormon Rocks Fire Station on Highway 138, just west of I-15. From San Bernardino: take I-15 North to the Highway 138 exit. From Los Angeles: take I-15 North approximately 60 miles. Free parking at the trailhead.

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

Spring (March–May) and Fall (September–November) for the most comfortable hiking temperatures. Summer can be hot but the elevation moderates the heat. The rocks are particularly dramatic at golden hour — sunrise and sunset paint the sandstone in deep reds and oranges. The Cajon Pass is accessible year-round; occasional winter snow dusts the formations beautifully.

⚰️ Riverside Cemetery

Denver's Oldest & Most Haunted Burial Ground — Est. 1876

Nestled on Denver's northeast border, Riverside Cemetery is the city's oldest active burial ground, established in 1876. Girded by the flaring flames and brimstone stench of the nearby Suncor refinery, this forgotten necropolis holds the bones of Denver's founders, outlaws, murder victims, and war heroes. The cemetery stopped selling new plots years ago, and the grass grows wild over the forgotten dead. Visitors report strange photographs, slamming doors on windless days, and apparitions drifting between the headstones at dusk. These are their stories.

James Archer

1824 — 1882
"He stood above the city he built — and still watches over it."

Season 1 — James Archer

👁️ THE WATCHER

James Archer's grave is unlike any other at Riverside Cemetery. A towering bronze statue of the man himself — hat in hand, coat buttoned against the Colorado wind — stands atop a massive stone pedestal, his cold metal eyes fixed permanently on the Denver skyline. He does not look down at the earth. He looks outward. Always outward.

Archer was a prominent Denver businessman and civic figure who helped shape the city's early commercial identity. He died in 1882, and his family commissioned this imposing monument as a testament to his legacy. But visitors who come at dusk report something unsettling: the statue seems to shift. Photographs taken from one angle show the figure facing east; taken minutes later from the same spot, it appears to face north. No one has ever caught the movement on camera.

Local legend holds that on the anniversary of his death, a figure in a long dark coat is seen walking the cemetery paths at midnight — always heading toward the statue, never away from it. Those who have followed the figure say it simply vanishes when it reaches the pedestal. The bronze man stands alone. Waiting.

Dogs brought to the cemetery consistently refuse to approach the Archer monument. They sit at a distance, ears flat, and will not move.

Nathan Baker & Frank

1843 — 1934

Season 2 & 3 — Nathan Baker & Edward

🐴 THE HORSE THAT WOULD NOT LEAVE

Nathan Baker's headstone is broken — the bottom half sheared clean off, as if something pushed up from below. The stone has been broken for decades. No one has repaired it. The cemetery staff say it keeps happening: they reset the stone, and within weeks it is broken again. The ground beneath it, they note, is always disturbed.

Baker was a Colorado pioneer and horse trader who reportedly loved his horse Frank more than any human being. He outlived Frank by eleven years, and by all accounts never recovered from the loss. He requested in his will that Frank be buried in the same plot. The request was denied. Frank was buried in a separate section of the cemetery. Baker was buried alone.

Visitors to the Baker grave at night report the sound of hooves on the gravel paths — a slow, deliberate walk that circles the headstone and then stops. No animal is ever found. Photographs taken near the grave frequently show a blurred shape at ground level, low and elongated, like a large animal lying down.

One groundskeeper, who worked at Riverside for thirty years, refused to enter the Baker section after dark. He never explained why. He simply would not go.

Captain Silas Soule

1838 — 1865
"He refused the order. He told the truth. He was shot dead at 26."

Season 9 — Silas Soule with Grave Background

⚖️ THE MURDERED WITNESS

On November 29, 1864, Colonel John Chivington ordered his troops to attack a peaceful encampment of Cheyenne and Arapaho people at Sand Creek, Colorado. It was not a battle. It was a massacre. Between 150 and 200 men, women, and children were killed. Captain Silas Soule refused to give the order to his men. He stood down. He watched. And then he testified.

Soule gave damning testimony against Chivington at the military inquiry. He named names. He described what he saw. He was the only officer who did. On April 23, 1865 — less than a month after his wedding — Silas Soule was shot dead in the street in Denver. The killer was never convicted. The case was dropped. Chivington was never charged.

Visitors to Soule's grave report a persistent cold spot, even on warm summer days. Some have heard what sounds like a man speaking quietly — not in distress, but urgently, as if giving testimony to someone just out of earshot. The words are never clear. The voice always stops when you get close.

Silas Soule was 26 years old. He had been married for 23 days.

Marie Contassot

1871 — 1894
"Morte à Denver le 28 Octobre 1894. Elle avait 23 ans."

Season 8 — Marie Contassot

👼 THE STRANGLER'S VICTIM

Marie Contassot was a young French woman, 23 years old, who died in Denver on October 28, 1894. Her grave is marked with a large stone angel — or what remains of one. The angel's arms are broken. Its face has been worn smooth by weather and, some say, by hands. The inscription is in French. She is far from home.

Denver in the 1890s had a strangler. Between 1893 and 1895, several young women were found dead under circumstances that were never fully explained. The cases were closed without convictions. Marie Contassot's death was officially recorded as illness. But her family, who traveled from France to bury her, reportedly told the cemetery staff she had been found in her room with marks on her throat.

The angel monument has been vandalized twice. Both times, the vandalism was discovered in the morning with no witnesses. Security footage from a nearby camera showed only static during the hours in question. The broken forearm of the angel was found placed upright in the ground several feet away, as if planted there deliberately.

The crematorium near her grave has a door that slams on windless days. Staff have stopped trying to latch it. It opens again within the hour.

Richard Whitsitt

1830 — 1881
"Denver's first duel. One man walked away. One did not."

Season 6 — Richard Whitsitt

🔫 THE DUELIST

Richard Whitsitt holds a grim distinction: he was killed in Denver's first recorded duel. In 1881, a dispute over a business deal escalated to pistols at dawn. Whitsitt was shot through the chest. His opponent walked away. The duel was technically illegal, but no charges were filed. Denver was still a frontier town. These things happened.

His grave is marked by a stone angel — but the angel is missing its right forearm. No one knows when it was lost or where it went. Cemetery records show the stone was intact as recently as 1940. By 1960, the arm was gone. There is no record of vandalism, no fragment found nearby. The arm simply ceased to exist.

Visitors who linger near the Whitsitt grave after sunset report hearing a single sharp crack — like a pistol shot — from somewhere in the cemetery. It happens once. It does not repeat. Those who turn toward the sound find nothing. Those who do not turn say the silence that follows feels like someone standing very close behind them.

A local historian who spent three nights at Riverside documenting paranormal activity reported the crack sound on all three nights, always between 11 PM and midnight, always from the direction of the Whitsitt grave.

Civil War Veterans

Sand Creek Massacre — 1864
"They followed orders. Some of them regretted it for the rest of their lives."

Civil War Veterans & Sand Creek Massacre

🪖 THE RESTLESS REGIMENT

In the northeast corner of Riverside Cemetery, rows of white military headstones stand in precise formation — the graves of Colorado's Civil War veterans, many of whom also served at Sand Creek. The stones are identical. The names are different. The silence here is different from the rest of the cemetery. It is not peaceful. It is held.

Several of the men buried here participated in the Sand Creek Massacre under Colonel Chivington. Some later testified that they had been ordered to fire. Some testified they had refused. The record is incomplete. The truth, as with most frontier violence, was buried alongside the men who knew it.

Visitors to this section at dawn — particularly in late November, near the anniversary of the massacre — report seeing figures moving between the headstones in the early morning mist. The figures are dressed in period military clothing. They do not respond when called to. They disappear when the sun fully rises.

One visitor photographed what appeared to be a full company of soldiers standing at attention between the headstones. When she looked up from her camera, the section was empty. The photograph was later analyzed by a photography expert who found no evidence of manipulation.

The Riverside River

The South Platte — Always
"She is always looking for something in the water. She never finds it."

The Riverside River — Where the Mother & Baby Spirits Are Seen

🌊 LA LLORONA OF DENVER

The South Platte River runs along the eastern edge of Riverside Cemetery, separating the dead from the living city beyond. The river is narrow here, shallow, unremarkable in daylight. At night, it is something else entirely.

For decades, residents of the neighborhoods bordering the cemetery have reported seeing a woman standing at the river's edge after midnight — always on the cemetery side, always facing the water. She is described consistently: long dark hair, pale dress, standing very still. When approached, she does not move. When called to, she does not respond. She is simply there, and then she is not.

Cemetery records from the 1890s reference the burial of a young mother and infant in the section nearest the river. The circumstances of their deaths are not recorded. The graves are unmarked. No one knows their names. But the woman at the water has been reported for over a hundred years, and the descriptions have never changed.

Children who live near the cemetery are told not to go near the river at night. They are not told why. They learn not to ask.

About Riverside Cemetery

Riverside Cemetery is Denver's oldest active cemetery, established in 1876. It is the final resting place of over 65,000 people, including Denver's founders, Civil War veterans, Sand Creek Massacre soldiers, and victims of frontier violence. The cemetery is maintained by a nonprofit preservation organization and is open to the public during daylight hours.
